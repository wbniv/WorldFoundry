# Mainline console controllers since the N64 / PS1 / Xbox eras

**Status:** reference catalog. Inputs to whatever downstream design decision wants to know what living-room controller hardware has actually existed.

## Scope

- Three platform holders: **Nintendo**, **Sony**, **Microsoft**.
- Starting point: **N64 (1996)** and **PS1 (1994/95)** — i.e. the "first analog-stick generation" through current. Microsoft only enters with the original Xbox (2001), so their column starts there.
- "Mainline" = first-party gamepads sold by the platform holder as the canonical play device for that console, including major hardware revisions and the platform's pro/elite variant. **Excluded**: peripheral controllers (light guns, fight sticks, dance pads, racing wheels), accessibility-specific controllers (Xbox Adaptive Controller — important but a separate category), Wii Remote attachments that aren't standalone (Nunchuk), and third-party gamepads.
- "Connection technology" column: each distinct hardware revision gets its own row when the supported connection set changes. Same-shell controllers that gained a new radio in revision (e.g. Xbox One launch → Xbox One S-era with Bluetooth) get separate rows. Chargeable wired play (USB used for *both* charge and input) is listed in addition to the wireless link.

## Caveats

- **MSRPs are launch USD MSRP** as published by the manufacturer at the time, recorded from memory of contemporary press and retail listings. Some I'm confident on (DualSense $69.99, Switch Pro $69.99); others (GameCube wired controller around $24.95 vs. $34.99) I'm fuzzier on — flagged in the notes column when uncertain. Real-time price floors after launch (e.g. Sony's repeated Black Friday DS4 markdowns) are not captured.
- **Production status** is best-effort circa 2026-04. "In production" means the manufacturer still ships new units through their direct storefront; "discontinued" means new manufacturing has stopped, even if some new-old-stock inventory remains. The line gets blurry for items like the DualShock 4 (still available new but at low volume).
- **Purchase links**: in-production items link to the manufacturer's storefront category page (more durable than deep product URLs that rot). Out-of-production items link to a stable eBay search pattern; user-side inspection is needed for condition + price. **No links are guessed at the deep-product level** — the URL patterns used here are well-known institutional storefronts and search endpoints.
- I have not personally verified every figure in this table against primary source documents. Treat as a working draft; flag corrections.

## Patterns worth noting

The catalog above is the data; this section is just what jumps out reading it.

- **Wireless became table-stakes in one generation.** N64 / PS1 / original Xbox: wired-only mainline. By 2006–2007 (Wii Remote, Sixaxis, Xbox 360 Wireless as default pack-in), wireless is the default and wired is the budget option.
- **Bluetooth ate every proprietary radio except Microsoft's.** Sony went Bluetooth from PS3 onward; Nintendo has been Bluetooth since the Wii Remote. Microsoft *added* Bluetooth on top of "Xbox Wireless" mid-Xbox-One (2016) but has never dropped the proprietary radio — every Xbox controller since carries both.
- **USB-C is the current charging consensus** (DualSense 2020, Series 2020, Switch Pro 2017 already, Edge / Elite Series 2). Micro-USB exited cleanly after the Xbox One mid-life and DS4.
- **Pro-tier pads are now a standard SKU** for both Sony (Edge) and Microsoft (Elite). Nintendo's pro tier has been the regular "Pro Controller" since 2012 (Wii U), with no further-tiered pro variant.
- **The most reliable secondhand-supply controllers** in this catalog are the Xbox 360 Wireless and the DualShock 2 — both shipped in absurd numbers, both still cheap and easy to find. The hardest are the Wii U GamePad (proprietary radio bound to the host system) and the WaveBird (genuine collector demand).
- **Pricing is remarkably stable in nominal dollars over 30 years.** The 1996 N64 controller was $29.99; the 2025 Switch 2 Pro is ~$84.99. Across three decades of inflation that is a real-dollar *decrease* — gamepads have gotten cheaper to make faster than they have gotten more capable.

## Out of scope / acknowledged gaps

- **Accessibility controllers.** The Xbox Adaptive Controller (2018) and Sony Access Controller (2023) are platform-holder first-party hardware but not mainline gamepads. They warrant their own catalog if anyone wants one.
- **Specialty peripherals.** GunCon (PS1/2), Steel Battalion controller (Xbox), DK Bongos (GameCube), Wii Balance Board, Guitar Hero / Rock Band controllers, Skylanders Portal, etc. — all platform-holder-blessed hardware, all out of scope here.
- **Third-party controllers under official program.** PowerA, Hori, Razer, etc. produce officially-licensed gamepads. None included in this catalog because the user specified mainline (first-party).
- **Region-specific revisions.** Japan often saw small color/SKU variants (translucent shells, anniversary editions) that aren't separately rowed here.
- **PC-first controllers.** Steam Controller (Valve, 2015), Steam Deck integrated controls, etc. — out of console-platform-holder scope by definition.
